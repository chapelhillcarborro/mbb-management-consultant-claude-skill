# M&A Case Study: Broadcom's Blocked Bid for Qualcomm — When the State Is the Deciding Party

## Snapshot

| | |
|---|---|
| **Acquirer** | Broadcom Limited — semiconductor group led by CEO Hock Tan, **domiciled in Singapore** at the time of the bid (mid-way through a redomiciliation to the United States), known for an aggressive, acquisition-led, cost-and-R&D-discipline operating model |
| **Target** | Qualcomm Incorporated — San Diego–based leader in mobile chipsets and, critically, in the cellular standard-essential-patent (SEP) licensing that underpins 3G/4G and the emerging **5G** standard |
| **Deal type** | **Unsolicited (hostile) bid** escalating into a **proxy contest** — Broadcom sought to replace the Qualcomm board to force a deal; no agreed merger agreement ever existed |
| **Announced** | November 2017 (initial ~$70/share, ~$105B equity); raised February 2018 to a "best and final" **$82/share** ($60 cash + $22 in Broadcom stock), ~**$117–121B** including debt — which would have been **the largest technology acquisition in history** |
| **Outcome** | **Prohibited by U.S. Presidential Executive Order on March 12, 2018**, acting on a CFIUS recommendation, on national-security grounds |
| **Reverse termination fee offered** | ~$8B (Broadcom dangled it to pressure Qualcomm to negotiate) |

This is the case to study for the theme that **no amount of price, financing, or shareholder support can close a deal the state decides to veto** — the foreign-investment / national-security screening dynamic. It is doubly instructive because the intervention came *before any deal was even agreed*, while the target was still resisting: the screening regime reached a hostile bid at the proxy-fight stage. Read alongside `references/cross-border-ma-execution.md` §2.1 (FDI/national-security screening) and §2.2 (merger control).

## Industry & Transaction Context

By late 2017 Broadcom under Hock Tan had built one of the industry's most effective serial-acquisition machines, buying scale and then applying private-equity-style discipline — deep cost cuts and tight R&D prioritisation toward near-term, cash-generative programs. Qualcomm was a different kind of asset: its value lay less in fabs than in a licensing franchise over the patents essential to cellular standards, and in a leading position in the race to define **5G**. Qualcomm was simultaneously fighting a two-front war — a bruising royalty dispute with major customers and regulators, and its own pending ~$44B acquisition of NXP Semiconductors that still needed antitrust clearance in China.

Broadcom saw a target whose share price was depressed by those disputes and whose R&D spend it believed it could rationalise. That thesis — *buy the 5G leader and cut its investment* — was precisely what turned a commercial takeover into a national-security question.

## Deal Mechanics: A Hostile Bid Routed Through the Boardroom

With Qualcomm's board rejecting its overtures, Broadcom did not have a friendly agreement to execute. It pursued the only route available for a hostile acquirer of a widely-held US company: **win control of the board.** Broadcom nominated a slate to replace **six of Qualcomm's eleven directors** — a majority — to be voted on at Qualcomm's annual shareholder meeting scheduled for **March 6, 2018**. A friendly board would then, in theory, agree to the merger. The economics were tuned to pressure: an all-in "best and final" $82/share, majority-cash to de-risk shareholders, plus a large reverse termination fee signalling confidence it could clear regulators.

The mechanism matters for the lesson: because there was no signed merger agreement, there was also **no negotiated allocation of regulatory risk** (no hell-or-high-water covenant, no break-fee architecture, no long-stop date) — the very tools described in `references/spa-deal-terms-benchmarking.md` §8 and `references/cross-border-ma-execution.md` §2.2. Broadcom was exposed to a regulatory veto with none of the contractual scaffolding a friendly deal would have built.

## Strategic Rationale — and Why It Backfired

Broadcom's rationale was financial and operational: consolidate the mobile-chip supply chain, extract cost synergies, and reallocate Qualcomm's heavy R&D toward higher-return programs. The flaw was that the specific R&D Broadcom implied it would cut — **5G leadership** — was viewed in Washington as a strategic national asset. The concern articulated by CFIUS and the President was a chain of consequences: a foreign-domiciled acquirer cutting Qualcomm's 5G investment would weaken US influence over the global 5G standard, and the vacuum would be filled by China's **Huawei**, handing a geopolitical rival dominance in next-generation telecoms infrastructure. The very value lever of the acquirer's model became the trigger for the veto.

## The National-Security Intervention — Anatomy of an FDI Veto

This is the core of the case. The sequence is a textbook illustration of how foreign-investment screening actually bites:

- **A unilateral filing changed the game.** Rather than wait, **Qualcomm itself filed a voluntary notice to CFIUS in January 2018**, inviting review of a deal it opposed — a defensive use of the screening regime by a *target* against a hostile bidder. This is a strategic move worth remembering: the FDI regime can be a takeover defence.
- **Interim measures halted the timetable.** In early March 2018, CFIUS ordered Qualcomm to **postpone its March 6 shareholder meeting by 30 days** to allow the investigation to proceed — a standstill that froze the proxy contest before shareholders could vote. The screening authority reached *inside the corporate calendar*.
- **Domicile and ownership were central.** Broadcom's **Singapore domicile** (and, in the background, concern about its links and the destination of R&D) put it squarely within "foreign acquirer of a sensitive US business" territory. Broadcom's attempt to **redomicile to the United States** — which it completed in April 2018 — came **too late** to moot the review.
- **The remedy was prohibition, not conditions.** On **March 12, 2018**, the President issued an Executive Order **prohibiting the acquisition and the proxy solicitation outright**, and barring the Broadcom-nominated directors from standing. Unlike merger-control cases that often end in divestiture remedies, this national-security review ended in a flat block.

**The parallel merger-control thread.** Qualcomm's own acquisition of NXP — a separate transaction — needed clearance from China's competition authority (SAMR), which never came; Qualcomm walked away from NXP in July 2018, paying a $2B break fee. Two different state-control regimes (US national-security screening of Broadcom-Qualcomm; Chinese merger control of Qualcomm-NXP) shaped the fate of the same company within months, a vivid demonstration of `references/cross-border-ma-execution.md` §2's point that FDI screening and merger control are separate, independently fatal gates.

## Post-Transaction Outcomes

- **The deal died permanently.** Broadcom abandoned the bid days after the order. There was no revised structure that could cure a national-security prohibition of *this acquirer* buying *this target*.
- **Broadcom pivoted.** It completed its US redomiciliation and redirected its acquisition strategy toward enterprise software and infrastructure (a path that later included large software acquisitions), where national-security screening was far less acute.
- **Qualcomm remained independent** and went on to resolve its licensing disputes and retain its 5G position — the outcome the intervention was designed to protect.
- **The case reset expectations for tech M&A.** It signalled that semiconductor and critical-technology deals with any foreign-control dimension would face national-security scrutiny as a first-order gating item, foreshadowing the subsequent global tightening of FDI regimes.

## Lessons

1. **The state can be the deciding counterparty.** In sensitive sectors, the government is effectively a third party to the deal with a veto — and it need not offer a remedy or a price. Map foreign-investment screening on Day 1, before price, as a potential *kill* item (`cross-border-ma-execution.md` §2.1).
2. **The acquirer's own value thesis can be the trigger.** Broadcom's R&D-cutting model was its edge in every prior deal and its fatal liability here. Stress-test the deal thesis against how *regulators and governments* will perceive it, not only how shareholders will.
3. **Domicile and ownership are substantive, not cosmetic.** A foreign domicile put the deal in scope; a redomiciliation attempted mid-review was too slow. Ownership structure and country links must be assessed at the outset.
4. **Screening regimes can reach hostile, pre-agreement bids.** A target with no merger agreement to defend can still be protected — and a target can *weaponise* the regime by filing against its pursuer. The absence of a signed agreement also meant no negotiated regulatory-risk allocation to fall back on.
5. **FDI screening and merger control are separate, independently fatal gates.** The same company was shaped within months by a US national-security block and a Chinese antitrust non-clearance. Clear *every* gate in *every* relevant jurisdiction, and drive the timeline off the long pole (`cross-border-ma-execution.md` §2.2).
6. **Standstills bite on the corporate calendar.** Interim measures froze a shareholder vote. Regulatory timelines, not deal-team timelines, govern; build the long-stop date and interim-period plan around them.
