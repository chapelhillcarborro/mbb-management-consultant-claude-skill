# Governance Failure Case Study: The IL&FS Collapse

## Snapshot

| | |
|---|---|
| **Company** | Infrastructure Leasing & Financial Services (IL&FS) — a non-banking financial company (NBFC) structured as an infrastructure financing "shadow bank," with 256+ group entities (subsidiaries, JVs, associates) spanning infrastructure, financial services, and education |
| **Core failure** | Classic asset-liability mismatch: borrowing short-term (6–12 months) in wholesale debt markets to fund long-gestation infrastructure projects (10–15 year paybacks), while carrying a AAA credit rating that masked the mismatch |
| **Ownership** | "Quasi-sovereign" perception driven by large institutional shareholders including LIC and SBI, alongside foreign investors (Japan's Orix Corporation, Abu Dhabi Investment Authority) |
| **Trigger event** | IL&FS Transportation Networks missed repayment of a ₹450 crore inter-corporate deposit to SIDBI in June 2018 |
| **Scale** | Group debt of ~₹91,000 crore as of March 2018, including ~₹25,000 crore short-term; roughly ₹57,000 crore owed to public-sector banks |
| **Rating collapse** | AAA-rated through August 2018; downgraded to D (default) within weeks once the first missed payment became public — one of the fastest AAA-to-default falls in Indian credit history |
| **Resolution** | Government superseded the board via the National Company Law Tribunal in October 2018; a new board (chaired by Uday Kotak) ran a multi-year, asset-by-asset resolution process rather than a single sale or bailout |

This is the case to study for what an asset-liability mismatch actually looks like from the outside — a company can carry the highest possible credit rating right up until the moment its funding model physically cannot roll over, and by the time that becomes visible, the damage has already spread to every institution that trusted the rating.

## Context: The Infrastructure Financier Nobody Watched Closely

IL&FS built and financed some of India's highest-profile infrastructure assets — including the Chenani-Nashri tunnel, one of Asia's longest highway tunnels — by borrowing in liquid, short-duration debt markets (commercial paper, inter-corporate deposits, short-term bank facilities) and deploying that money into projects that wouldn't generate stable cash flow for a decade or more. This is a workable model only if the entity can continuously refinance its short-term liabilities as they mature — which is exactly what stopped happening once lenders lost confidence. Because IL&FS was structurally complex (a holding company sitting atop 256+ subsidiaries, joint ventures, and associates, many created to ring-fence or route capital for specific projects), the actual scale of the group's leverage and cross-obligations was extremely difficult for any single lender or rating agency to see in full.

## Mechanism: How a AAA Rating Missed a ₹91,000 Crore Problem

The mismatch itself wasn't a secret from IL&FS management — it was the operating model. What failed was the early-warning system that was supposed to price that risk. Rating agencies (ICRA and CARE among them) maintained top-tier ratings on IL&FS debt instruments almost until the point of default, giving institutional lenders — mutual funds, provident funds, banks — no signal to reduce exposure. When ICRA finally downgraded IL&FS Transportation Networks' paper from AAA to AA+ following the June 2018 missed SIDBI payment, and then rapidly further to default grade as more obligations came due, the market had essentially zero lead time to reposition. SEBI later fined all three rating agencies involved ₹1 crore each for the failure — a token penalty relative to the systemic damage, and a useful data point on how weakly rating-agency liability was calibrated at the time relative to the reliance markets placed on those ratings.

## Contagion: Why One NBFC's Default Became Everyone's Problem

The default itself was a manageable-sized event; the panic it triggered was not. Once IL&FS defaulted, mutual funds and banks — many of which held IL&FS commercial paper or had lent to comparable NBFCs — stopped rolling over short-term funding to the entire NBFC and housing-finance sector on the assumption that "if IL&FS could hide this, who else is hiding it." Borrowing costs for healthy NBFCs spiked, credit to small businesses, vehicle buyers, and consumers dried up almost overnight, and the freeze meaningfully dragged on GDP growth for the following several quarters — a textbook illustration of how a single shadow-bank failure can propagate into a systemic liquidity crunch when the broader market can't distinguish a well-run NBFC from a mismatched one.

## Resolution: Government Supersession, Not a Bailout

Rather than injecting public capital to keep IL&FS solvent, the government took the more surgical route of superseding the existing board via the NCLT in October 2018 and installing a new board tasked purely with orderly resolution: selling viable assets, restructuring or winding down non-viable ones, and repaying creditors according to a court-approved waterfall — a multi-year process rather than a single transaction. In parallel, RBI tightened liquidity risk management and asset-liability management (ALM) disclosure norms for NBFCs, effectively mandating the granular liquidity-gap reporting that would have surfaced this mismatch years earlier had it existed in 2016.

## Lessons

For due diligence and credit work, the transferable point is that a credit rating is an opinion about probability of default at a point in time, not a real-time monitor of liquidity structure — anyone underwriting exposure to a financial intermediary needs to independently map the tenor mismatch between what it borrows and what it lends, rather than deferring to the rating on the instrument. For anyone advising on financial-sector regulation or risk, the case is a clean illustration of contagion mechanics: the market's inability to differentiate between similar-looking institutions after one failure is itself a risk that has to be modeled, not an irrational overreaction to be dismissed. And for anyone structuring or reviewing a complex group with 200+ legal entities, IL&FS is the reference case for why entity-count and cross-holding complexity is itself a red flag worth pricing — not because complexity is inherently fraudulent, but because it is the single biggest obstacle to anyone outside management actually seeing the group's true risk position in time to act on it.
