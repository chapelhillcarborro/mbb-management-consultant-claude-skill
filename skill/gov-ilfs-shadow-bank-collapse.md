# Governance Failure Case Study: The Harshad Mehta Securities Scam

## Snapshot

| | |
|---|---|
| **Central figure** | Harshad Mehta — Bombay Stock Exchange broker, previously known for outsized bullish calls, dubbed the "Big Bull" during the run-up |
| **Core failure** | Exploited settlement-system loopholes in the interbank government-securities market to divert bank funds into the stock market using forged bank receipts |
| **Mechanism** | "Ready forward" (repo-style) deals between banks, settled on trust via paper Bank Receipts (BRs) rather than physical securities transfer — Mehta's network issued BRs against securities that didn't exist or were already pledged elsewhere |
| **Scale of diversion** | An estimated ₹4,024 crore siphoned from public-sector banks into equity markets via fraudulent BRs and rigged ready-forward transactions |
| **Market impact** | BSE Sensex rose from ~1,000 (early 1991) to a peak of 4,467 on April 23, 1992 — over 300% in roughly 15 months — before crashing to ~2,500 within weeks of exposure, wiping out an estimated ₹1,000 billion (~₹1,00,000 crore) in market capitalization |
| **Exposure** | Broken by journalist Sucheta Dalal in *The Times of India*, April 23, 1992 — the same day the Sensex hit its peak |
| **Legacy** | Directly triggered the transformation of SEBI into a statutory regulator with real enforcement power, the creation of the NSE as a transparent screen-based exchange, and the shift to dematerialized, electronically settled securities |

This is the case to study for how a scam built on a settlement-system technicality — not a fake product or a fake company — can move an entire national market by hundreds of percent, and for how a genuinely broken piece of market infrastructure (trust-based paper settlement) can look invisible right up until someone routes ₹4,000+ crore through it.

## Context: A Settlement System Built on Trust, Not Verification

In the early 1990s, the interbank market for government securities ran on "ready forward" deals — short-term, repo-like transactions where one bank would temporarily sell securities to another for cash, agreeing to repurchase them later. The problem was mechanical: actual securities often didn't change hands for each transaction. Instead, banks exchanged Bank Receipts (BRs) — paper instruments confirming that the selling bank held the underlying securities on the buyer's behalf. This worked as a convenience precisely because no one was independently verifying, deal by deal, that the securities named on each BR actually existed and weren't already committed elsewhere. Mehta, operating as a broker with deep relationships across this market, exploited that verification gap directly.

## Mechanism: Fake Paper, Real Cash

Mehta's network arranged for banks to issue BRs that were either backed by securities that had already been pledged in other transactions or, in the more aggressive versions, backed by nothing at all — pure fabrications that functioned as unsecured, undisclosed loans. Banks handed over real cash against these fictitious guarantees, and that cash flowed through Mehta into the stock market, where he used it to take large, concentrated positions in specific stocks, most visibly cement and capital-goods names like ACC (which moved from roughly ₹200 to roughly ₹9,000 during the run-up). The buying pressure this generated created a self-reinforcing story: rising prices attracted more retail and institutional money into the same stocks, which pushed prices higher still, which made the "Big Bull" thesis look validated — right up until the underlying bank funding it depended on was exposed as fictitious.

## The Crash and Its True Cost

Once Sucheta Dalal's April 23, 1992 exposé made the mechanism public, the ready-forward market froze, the fabricated BRs became worthless overnight, and the stocks that had been propped up by the diverted cash gave back their gains just as fast as they'd made them — the Sensex fell roughly 44% from its peak within weeks. The public-sector banking sector absorbed direct losses in the low thousands of crores from the fake securities on its own books, but the more lasting cost was to retail investor participation and trust: an entire generation of Indian retail investors who had entered the market during the 1991–92 boom exited at a loss and stayed out of equities for years afterward.

## Regulatory Legacy: The Reform That Modern SEBI Was Built On

The scam's real significance is what it triggered rather than what it stole. Prior to 1992, SEBI existed but had limited statutory teeth; the scam directly led to legislative amendments giving it full powers to register and regulate intermediaries, investigate market abuse, and impose penalties. The BSE's opaque, floor-based, broker-controlled trading model — which had made this kind of concentrated manipulation possible in the first place — was directly challenged by the creation of the National Stock Exchange as a transparent, screen-based, order-matched alternative, which within a few years pulled enough volume away from the BSE to force it to modernize as well. Physical share certificates and paper-based settlement were phased out in favor of dematerialized holdings and electronic settlement (the NSDL was established in 1996), and brokers were removed as intermediaries in interbank government-securities settlement, replaced by direct, RBI-tracked electronic transfer through the Subsidiary General Ledger system — closing the exact loophole Mehta had exploited. Mehta himself faced dozens of criminal and civil cases, was barred from the securities market, and died in judicial custody in 2001 with most of the litigation against him still unresolved.

## Lessons

The transferable lesson for anyone assessing market or counterparty risk is that a settlement mechanism built on trust rather than independent verification is a systemic vulnerability regardless of how long it has worked without incident — the absence of a prior failure is not evidence the control is sound, only evidence no one has yet had the incentive and access to exploit it at scale. For anyone evaluating a rapid, narrative-driven price run in any asset, the Sensex's 300%-in-15-months move is a reminder that a compelling story and real buying pressure can coexist with completely fabricated underlying capital — price action alone never confirms the legitimacy of the capital behind it. And at the policy and institutional level, this case is the clearest illustration in Indian market history of how a market infrastructure failure, once exposed, can force through structural reforms (SEBI's statutory powers, NSE, dematerialization) that decades of ordinary policy advocacy could not have achieved on their own.
