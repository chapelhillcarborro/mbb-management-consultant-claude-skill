# M&A Case Study: Apollo's Merger with Athene Holdings

## Snapshot

| | |
|---|---|
| **Acquirer** | Apollo Global Management — global alternative asset manager (private equity, credit, real assets), focused on long-term capital deployment and yield generation |
| **Target** | Athene Holding — leading US retirement-services and annuity provider, backed by a large, high-quality investment portfolio, ~$150 billion of annuity float |
| **Deal type** | Full-integration merger — Apollo already held an affiliated ~35% stake in Athene going in; this transaction absorbed the rest |
| **Announced** | March 8, 2021 |
| **Closed** | January 1, 2022 |
| **Deal value** | All-stock, ~$11 billion for the remaining stake — 1.149 Apollo common shares per Athene Class A share, a ~16% premium to Athene's pre-deal price |
| **Advisors** | Apollo: Goldman Sachs and PJT Partners. Athene: Morgan Stanley and Evercore |

This is a genuinely different M&A archetype from the other cases in this set: not a contested strategic acquisition or a hostile fight, but an affiliate consolidating a structurally undervalued, under-owned asset it already partially controlled. It's the case to study for value-investing logic applied to deal pricing, and for how index/passive-ownership gaps create real acquisition arbitrage.

## Industry & Transaction Context

Apollo and Athene were not arm's-length strangers before this deal — Apollo had helped found and had long managed money for Athene since 2009, and had already increased its economic stake to roughly 35% through a preceding 2021 transaction: a $1.55 billion equity-for-equity exchange (35.5 million Athene shares issued at a premium) paired with $350 million in cash, executed without new debt, which also added $1 billion of excess capital to Athene. The January 2022 merger absorbed the remaining ownership, creating a fully integrated retirement-and-asset-management platform with combined AUM north of $500 billion (up from Apollo's pre-deal $455B) and access to Athene's full $150B annuity float on Apollo's own balance sheet rather than through a fee-paying management relationship.

Apollo's own perpetual-capital AUM had grown roughly threefold over the prior five years heading into the deal, with Athene consistently the largest single contributor to that growth — meaning the "acquisition" was in large part Apollo formalizing ownership of the growth engine it had already built.

## Why This Was a High-Conviction Bet: The Valuation Arbitrage

This is the analytically interesting part of the case. Athene, despite genuinely strong fundamentals, was trading at just 1.1x book value — roughly 30% below insurance peers — while delivering a 13% ROE and $8.5 billion of net investment income in FY2021. Between 2017 and 2019, Athene's adjusted operating EPS grew from $3.77 to $6.58 (a 13% CAGR) and adjusted book value per share grew 38% (from $35.89 to $49.50) — yet over the same window, its stock price fell 21% (from $53.15 to $42.00), and its P/E multiple was cut roughly in half (10x to 5x) while its P/B multiple fell 43% (1.5x to 0.8x). Strong fundamentals, weak market recognition — the two lines were moving in opposite directions.

The explanation was structural rather than operational: Athene had unusually low passive-index and mutual-fund ownership relative to insurance peers — about 11% passive ownership versus roughly 22% for the broader S&P 500 insurance group, and about 63% long-only/mutual-fund ownership versus roughly 82% for peers. Limited index inclusion and muted mutual-fund interest kept Athene off many institutional buy-lists regardless of its actual balance-sheet quality — a genuine institutional-visibility gap rather than a fundamentals problem, and exactly the kind of gap that creates a real acquisition arbitrage: you can buy quality assets at a structural discount if you're one of the few buyers actually paying attention to them.

That's also why Apollo's own 16% exchange premium reads as unusually disciplined rather than cheap or hostile: precedent insurance-sector takeover premiums in the years before this deal ran from roughly 9% up to 51%, with a mean around 16% and a median closer to 30%. Apollo priced right at the low end of the historical range — appropriate given it wasn't bidding against anyone else for an asset it already effectively controlled, and given the all-stock structure conserved cash and limited near-term dilution while preserving upside for a governance-led re-rating post-merger. The market's initial reaction was skeptical rather than celebratory: unusually high share-trading volume around the announcement reflected real investor doubt about whether the promised integration synergies would actually materialize.

## Deal Mechanics & Structure

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Prior step-up (2021) | $1.55B equity-for-equity + $350M cash, no new debt, raised Apollo's stake to ~35% |
| Excess capital created for Athene | ~$1 billion |
| Share buyback authorization added | $600 million |
| Exchange ratio (final merger) | 1.149 Apollo shares per Athene Class A share |
| Premium to Athene's pre-deal price | ~16% |
| Implied discount to Athene's own stated NAV (~$23) | ~20% |
| Combined AUM | $455B (Apollo standalone) → $548B combined |

The 16% premium against a ~20% discount to Athene's own book/NAV is the whole thesis in one line: Apollo paid a premium to the trading price while still buying the underlying assets below their own stated net asset value.

## Strategic Rationale

The integration thesis rested on turning Athene's annuity float from a passive insurance balance sheet into an active origination engine for Apollo's credit business. A few of the concrete mechanics: Athene generated $37.0 billion in gross organic inflows in 2021 alone (including $13.8B from pension-group annuities), averaging roughly $8.4 billion annually in net organic inflows in the three years pre-merger and scaling toward $27.5 billion in gross inflows by the third year shown — with net inflows compounding from $4.3B to $6.5B to $14.4B across that same period, evidence of efficient gross-to-net retention rather than just growing headline volume. Athene's asset mix ran roughly 47% asset-backed, materially higher than the ~24% average for top-200 pension plans, reflecting a genuine origination edge in funding long-duration annuity liabilities with yield-oriented private credit — exactly the kind of asset Apollo's own credit platform was built to underwrite, syndicate, and fee-wrap.

The ACRA/ADIP sidecar vehicle program (permanent-capital vehicles co-investing alongside Athene) added further scale: potential for roughly $60 billion of additional premium float, with ADIP II alone raising $6 billion in commitments, and Apollo targeting $15 billion of deployable capital over five years off the back of the combined float.

By Q3 2021, Athene's trailing-twelve-month ROE reached approximately 18.9%, well above its own decade average of roughly 11.6% — direct evidence that the capital-light annuity/private-credit combination was already outperforming Athene's own historical baseline even before full integration closed.

## Post-Transaction Outcomes

Apollo's own framing of the deal, some quarters out, centered on four outcomes: a genuine permanent-capital ecosystem established (recurring, duration-matched capital rather than fund-cycle-dependent AUM); a growth-oriented re-rating of Apollo's own earnings multiple as the market began pricing in the annuity float's durability; a scalable, self-funding growth engine, since Athene's recurring inflows fund Apollo's origination pipeline without needing repeated capital raises; and — the part that validates the original arbitrage thesis — improved governance and a larger public float that made passive-index inclusion and broader institutional ownership genuinely more plausible post-merger, directly targeting the ~11% passive-ownership and ~20% long-only-ownership gaps identified pre-deal.

Apollo has also used the platform to shift its own business model, from a traditional "vanilla fund investments, passive LP relationships" structure toward what it calls a holistic, LP-centric ecosystem spanning funds, co-investments, direct deals, strategic partnerships, capital solutions, knowledge sharing, sidecars, and portfolio solutions — using Athene's permanent capital base as the foundation for a broader shift in how the firm raises and deploys money.

## Lessons

The transferable technique here is a genuine value-investing framework applied to M&A pricing: when a target's fundamentals (earnings growth, book-value growth, ROE) are clearly outrunning its stock-price performance, check ownership structure before assuming the market has correctly priced the business — a passive/index-ownership gap is a structural, not fundamental, explanation for underperformance, and it's a gap an acquirer can specifically underwrite (and later close) rather than just hope resolves on its own.

The second lesson is about premium discipline in affiliate or insider-adjacent deals: because Apollo wasn't competing against another bidder for Athene, it could price at the low end of precedent premiums rather than needing to win an auction — a genuinely different playbook from every contested deal in this case set, and a reminder that "premium paid" should always be read relative to how much competitive pressure the acquirer was actually under, not as a standalone number.
